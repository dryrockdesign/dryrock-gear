User Guide & Methodology

Dryrock Design — Polymer Gear Design Studio

This studio sizes and verifies thermoplastic spur gears following the framework of VDI 2736 Part 2 (load-carrying capacity of cylindrical thermoplastic gears), the de-facto reference for plastic gearing. Metal standards (ISO 6336, AGMA 2001) are not directly applicable because polymers exhibit low temperature-dependent modulus, viscoelastic load sharing, frictional self-heating and wear-dominated failure.

Scope & honesty. Geometric and force computations are exact. Form factors (YFa, YSa) are interpolated from ISO/VDI tables; profile-shift strengthening is credited via an approximate form-factor correction (≈ −18 %·x on YFa). Thermal, PV and wear outputs are indicative estimates from empirical coefficients (μ, HV, Rth, kw) you must supply from VDI 2736 tables or supplier data. Material library values are order-of-magnitude and must be verified.

1 · Geometry & nomenclature

For module m, teeth z, shift x: pitch d = m·z; base d_b = d·cos α; tip dₐ = m·(z+2+2x); root d_f = m·(z−2.5+2x). The transverse contact ratio εα should exceed 1.2. For a pair, profile shift sets the working pressure angle via inv α_w = inv α + 2 tan α (x₁+x₂)/(z₁+z₂) and centre distance a_w = a·cos α / cos α_w.

2 · Tangential load & kinematics

Fₜ = 2000·T₁ / d₁ (N). Pitch-line velocity v = π·d₁·n₁/60000 (m/s). Power P = T₁·2π·n₁/60 (W). For a pair, output n₂ = n₁/i, T₂ = T₁·i·η, where i = z₂/z₁ and η is the mesh efficiency from the friction-loss estimate.

3 · Tooth-root bending (failure: root crack)

σ_F = (Fₜ/(b·m))·Y_Fa·Y_Sa·Y_ε, with Yε=0.25+0.75/εα. Computed for both pinion and gear (the gear has more teeth, hence lower form factor and stress). Safety S_F = σ_FP/σ_F, σFP = σFlim·Kprint. Aim S_F ≥ 1.5–2.0 for printed polymer given data scatter.

4 · Tooth-flank contact (failure: pitting / wear)

σ_H = Z_E·Z_H·Z_ε·√(Fₜ·(u+1)/(b·d₁·u)). The elasticity factor ZE is small for polymers, so flank stress is usually low; plastic-pair flank failure is dominated by wear and temperature, not classical pitting.

5 · Frictional heating & flank temperature

VDI 2736 makes the verification temperature-based. Mesh loss P_VZ ≈ μ·H_V·P; indicative flank temperature θ_fla ≈ θ₀ + P_VZ·R_th. Rth depends strongly on housing/airflow — treat θ as a screening figure. Keep it well below the polymer's continuous service temperature.

6 · Profile shift & auto-balance

Positive pinion shift prevents undercut below z_min = 2(1−x)/sin²α (≈17 at 20°). In Pair mode the Auto-balance button scans x₁ (with x₂ = −x₁ to preserve centre distance) to equalise pinion and gear root safety — the classic way to make the weak pinion as durable as the gear.

7 · Root fillet

The sharp root corner is the primary crack-initiation site, especially in layered FDM parts. The root fillet ρ (entered as a fraction of module, standard basic-rack value ρfP = 0.38·m) rounds this corner, lowering the stress-concentration factor. Larger fillets help printed gears most.

8 · Hub, bore & keyway

Round, D-profile, hex and parallel-key (DIN 6885-1) bores are supported; key width and hub depth auto-select from shaft Ø. A radial fit clearance enlarges the printed bore for a slip/press fit. For printed parts prefer a D-flat or hex over a keyway, whose corners are fracture-prone.

9 · 3D-printing notes

FDM gears are anisotropic; layer lines printed flat create weak planes in the loaded root. Kprint knocks the permissible stress down accordingly. PA / PA-CF are the strongest practical printed gear materials; PETG/ASA mid-range; PLA brittle and low-temperature. Increase module, face width and fillet; minimise running temperature.
